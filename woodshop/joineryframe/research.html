Wood Joinery Frame

Research

Step 1: My research about various different kinds of wood joinery.

Name: Mitre (or miter) joint

Definition: Two pieces of wood to be joined are cut across the main surface, usually at a 45° angle, to form a corner, usually a 90° angle.

Best used for: Easy to make; commonly used for picture frames or furniture.

Weaknesses: Not a very strong joint.

Name: Half lap joint

Definition: Two pieces of wood, each reduced to half of their thickness where they lap over each other. This provides a joint with plenty of gluing surface.

Best used for: It’s good for furniture pieces, such as the inside of dressers or desks where a drawer will be installed, adding sturdiness to the internal structure without any additional height.

Weaknesses: Thin pieces of wood may be weakened when cutting out half of the material for the joint, losing their structural integrity.

Name: Dovetail joint

Definition: Made from a series of trapezoidal pins cut to extend from the end of one board that interlock with a series of trapezoidal tails cut into the end of another board.

Best used for: It has very high resistance to being pulled apart. It’s commonly used to join the sides and front of a drawer.

Weaknesses: Difficult to make well.

Name: Butt joint

Definition: Two pieces of wood are joined by simply placing their ends together.

Best used for: The simplest joint to make. Used in picture frames, cabinets, boxes, and drawers.

Weaknesses: The weakest joint, relying on glue alone on only one surface to hold it together. Can be reinforced through nails, screws, dowels, etc.

Name: Pocket screw or hole

Definition: A hole is drilled at an angle which forms a pocket for the screw that joins the two pieces of wood.

Best used for: A simple yet strong way of joining two pieces of wood. Can be used in cabinets, bookshelves, tables, and chairs.

Weaknesses: The angled holes are very visible and are hard to cover up.

Name: Mortise-and-tenon

Definition: The tenon, formed on the end of one piece of wood, fits into the mortise, a rectangular hole cut into the other piece.

Best used for: A very strong and stable joint used in lots of furniture and other woodworking projects.

Weaknesses: Difficult to create well.

Name: Tongue and groove

Definition: Each piece has a groove cut all along one edge, and a thin, deep ridge (the tongue) on the opposite edge, causing them to fit closely.

Best used for: Flooring, paneling, tables, and other flat surfaces.

Weaknesses: You can’t glue very thin panels, and it can be hard to repair.

Name: Dowel joint

Definition: Round wooden pins (dowels) are used to reinforce a butt joint.

Best used for: A strong joint commonly used in furniture such as drawers or dressers.

Weaknesses: Difficult to create well.

Name: Dado

Definition: A slot or trench cut into the surface of a piece of wood in which another piece is secured.

Best used for: Often used for cabinets, shelves, and other carpentry.

Weaknesses: Can be hard to create well.

Name: Biscuit

Definition: Oval-shaped pieces of wood (called biscuits) are inserted and glued into slots cut into two pieces of wood.

Best used for: A strong joint used for furniture and joining two panels or boards together.

Weaknesses: Not the strongest joint and requires a special tool to create.

Name: Rabbet (or rebate)

Definition: A recess or groove cut into the edge of a piece of wood, used to form a joint with another piece.

Best used for: A simple joint often used in carpentry such as drawers, cabinets, and lighter items like frames.

Weaknesses: Not a very strong joint.
