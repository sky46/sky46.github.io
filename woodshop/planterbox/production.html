Production

Step 3: Production


I began by picking out my pieces. I tried my best to pick flat, straight ones as many were warped or had imperfections. I started with the pieces for the main sides and the bottom. There is a large pile in the tech room of pieces to choose from, but I needed pieces that were 1 inch thick, while the pieces in the tech room were only Â¾ of an inch thick. Mr. Doherty brought pieces from another room, which were all pretty flat and long, and thick enough for my box.

This time, I didn't really need to do any drawing or marking on the pieces because many had the same dimensions and the table and mitre saws could cut them precisely and uniformly without the help of drawings/measurements on the wood. I basically just measured the large pieces and figured out how many of what kind of piece I would be able to cut from it based off of the dimensions.

During this time, I also picked out some of the pieces for my trim, but I later realized that it was a different kind of wood, so I ended up having to redo those pieces from another piece of wood that was the same type as the rest of my box.

I first used the table saw to cut all the long pieces to the correct width, then used the mitre saw to cut the resulting boards to the correct lengths. Since many of the pieces were the same, I first measured and cut one to the correct length, and then used that initial one to line up and cut the rest of the pieces to the exact same length. I left a little bit of excess (e.g. if I needed a 12 inch long piece, I'd cut it to 12.25 inches) on the length of the pieces to leave some room for inevitable error during the gluing process, after which I could still cut the edge flat while still keeping the correct length.

Mr. Doherty did the cutting with the table saw. He adjusted the height of the table saw and the distance of the fence to what I needed, then pushed it through with his hands and then a wooden push stick, keeping a straight edge flat with the fence. For the mitre saw, I measured and marked using a pencil the desired length (as I said, leaving some excess), then pushed the wood flat against the guide, lined up the saw blade (making sure to cut on the outside of the line to account for the thickness of the blade), held it in place, and cut the initial piece. I then used this initial piece to line up and cut all the pieces that were the same length, and then repeated this process for subsequent pieces.

After that, I scored each side that would be glued using a chiselâthat is, I made little Xâs on the wood using the sharp point of the chisel. I believe the purpose of this was to make the glue be absorbed and adhere to the wood better and more easily. I tried my best not to go all the way to the edges to avoid damaging the sides of the wood.

Then came the gluing itself. I placed the wood into 2 parallel loosened clamps. One by one, I took a piece, applied some glue along the side to be glued using the bottle, spread it evenly using a short, thin piece of used excess wood, repeated for the other side if necessary, and replaced it into the clamps. Once all the pieces had glue, I tightened the clamps at the same time so that it would be even. I made sure to keep the pieces flat and properly aligned, as well as putting the clamps on the very edges of the wood, to prevent unevenness and splitting. I wiped the extra glue being squeezed out on the top and bottom with a paper towel, and then put 'one more clamp in the middle and a weight or two on top of the wood to keep it flat. I repeated this process, doing one face per day (we didnât have that many clamps). I think Mr. Doherty removed the wood from the clamps later in the day after the class.

Once each face was finished gluing, I scraped off any glue I could using an upside-down chisel (upside-down to avoid damaging the wood), then cut the sides flat using the table saw. I first cut off a tiny bit on one side to get a straight edge, then lined it up using the straight edge and cut the other side to the desired width. The excess length I had left on the pieces left me with just enough to keep the correct dimensions. Between classes, Mr. Doherty put the faces through the thickness planer in the other room to smooth out the pieces and get them all the same thickness.
